A short history of Upminster

Evidence of a Romano-British farmstead, said to have been occupied during the 1st and 3rd centuries, was discovered west of Corbets Tey during gravel digging in Upminster in 1962. In medieval Upminster, settlement seems to have been in three clusters: the village itself, and the hamlets of Hacton and Corbets Tey, which expanded slowly, with a few scattered farms being built at the north and south ends of the parish. Although it was completely rebuilt in 1861-62, St Laurence's church on the Corbets Tey Road retains a tower with stonework dating to around 1200, and it is believed a church may have existed on or near this site as early as the 7th century.
There were three manors recorded in Upminster in 1086, the largest of which, originally known as the manor of Upminster, was later known as Gaynes, or Engaynes, after Vitalis Engayne who acquired it in 1214. New Place began as part of the Gaynes estate in 1556, but in 1775 a new house was built on the site by Sir James Esdaile, who developed several of the houses in Upminster. He added a clock turret, which came from Woolwich Arsenal, to the roof of the stable block. It houses a clock made in 1774 by famous London clock maker, Edward Tutet. New Place itself was demolished in 1924, but the stable block remained and is known today as the Clockhouse. The third manor of Upminster, also known as Waltham Upminster and Upminster Hall, was gifted to the college (later abbey) of Waltham Holy Cross in the 9th century by Earl Harold. It is said that today the ghosts of the monks can be glimpsed in a tree walk in the nearby playing fields that were part of the estate! The manor saw many changes in ownership over the years until 1686 when it was bought by Andrew Branfil, and it remained in his family until the early 20th century. Today it is home to the Upminster Golf Club.
The economic history of Upminster has chiefly been that of mixed agriculture, with an upsurge in market-gardening in the 19th century. This agricultural heritage is evidenced in two fine monuments still to be seen today. As well as Upminster Windmill of 1803, there is Upminster Barn (also known as Upminster 'Tithe' Barn), which is thought to have been built in the early 15th century, and was used as a threshing barn. Both are now heritage attractions open to the public.

Some People and Places in the Upminster Story

Upminster Windmill stands on Upminster Hill and was built by local farmer James Nokes in 1803. It is the largest smock mill remaining in the London area, and considered to be one of the finest examples. It is now open to the public as a heritage attraction.
Alice Perrers lived at Gaynes Manor, Upminster. She died there in 1400 after a life of great notoriety. She was married to Sir William of Windsor, Viceroy of Ireland, but was also the notorious mistress of Edward III. Her influence over the king led to accusations of witchcraft in 1376.
William Derham was vicar of Upminster from 1689 to 1735. A natural philosopher, and the author of many research papers, he is credited with producing the earliest, reasonably accurate estimate of the speed of sound. He became a fellow of the Royal Society in 1702, and lived in High House until his death in 1735.
Lady Edna Clarke Hall, an artist and poet, lived in Great House, a 16th century farmhouse in Upminster. She studied at the Slade School of Art in 1895-9 and had several exhibitions from 1901 onwards. Several of her drawings are held in the Tate Gallery. She died in 1979, aged 100.
Henria Williams lived in Upminster and was active in the Suffragette movement. In a march on the House of Commons in 1910 there was a disturbance that would become known as 'Black Friday'. Many protesters, including Williams, were badly injured by police. Sadly, Williams died suddenly two months later from heart failure.
Ian Dury, painter, musician and actor was born in Harrow in 1942, but after his parents separated he moved with his mother to Cranham. He reflected his teenage Teddy boy life around Upminster in the song 'Upminster Kids,' and titled one of his albums 'Lord Upminster'.


Did you know?

...that High House, built in 1580, was home to many notable people including Major Frederick Howard, who died at the Battle of Waterloo? Lord Byron, the poet, was a friend of Howard's, and when the house was demolished in 1935, the parade of shops built on the site was named 'Byron Parade' in recognition of his visits.
